Material notes

How a polo
earns its hand.

A practical guide to cotton, knit, weight and finishing—because premium feel is never explained by one number alone.

Begin with the whole cloth

A premium polo fabric should feel composed on first touch and remain convincing after hours of wear. GSM matters, but fibre quality, yarn, knit structure, finishing and recovery decide what that weight becomes.

A dense cloth made with poor yarn can feel stiff and trap heat. A lighter fabric made from well-selected cotton can feel smooth, stable and remarkably refined. At G&G Atelier, material language is intended to be specific: explain each construction rather than hiding behind the word “luxury.”

Six quality signals

Read a polo
beyond the label.

01

Cotton staple

Staple describes fibre length. Longer, more uniform cotton fibres can be spun into smoother yarn with fewer protruding ends, supporting a cleaner surface and better resistance to fuzz. Staple is important, but the spinning and knitting still matter.

02

Piqué or jersey

Piqué has a raised, cellular face that encourages airflow and gives the classic polo its structure. Jersey is flatter and often softer against the skin. Neither is automatically superior; the choice should suit the intended drape, climate and formality.

03

Mercerisation

Mercerisation is a controlled cotton treatment that can increase lustre, colour affinity and dimensional stability. Done well, it creates a polished, cool hand. It does not replace good fibre, and the finish should not feel artificially slick.

04

GSM and density

Grams per square metre measures fabric mass, not quality. A 220–240 GSM polo can feel substantial and drape cleanly, yet two fabrics with the same GSM may differ greatly in breathability, softness and recovery.

05

Collar recovery

The collar frames the face and often reveals quality early. A stable rib should return after gentle stretching, sit cleanly at the placket and resist rolling. Construction, seam balance and laundering all affect its life.

06

Finish and colour

Even dyeing, controlled shrinkage and a surface free from unnecessary coating help a polo age gracefully. Printed polos also need the motif scale, colour penetration and hand-feel to work with the base cloth.

The weight question

Why 220 GSM can feel premium.

At this weight, a cotton polo can have enough body to hold an athletic silhouette while remaining wearable in many settings.

The result depends on how the weight is built. Compact yarn, balanced knitting and sensible finishing can produce a smooth fall and reduced transparency. Dense, inflexible construction can produce the opposite. For the complete explanation, read why 220 GSM polo shirts feel premium.

  • 01Hold the fabric to light and assess evenness rather than assuming opacity equals quality.
  • 02Compress the cloth gently; it should recover without remaining crushed or board-like.
  • 03Inspect the collar, placket and shoulder seams where stability is most visible.

Care is part of
the material story.

Always follow the care label supplied with the finished garment. In general, gentle handling protects colour, shape and surface.

  • Wash thoughtfully: turn printed polos inside out and use the temperature stated on the label.
  • Dry with restraint: reshape while damp and avoid unnecessary high heat.
  • Store calmly: fold heavier knits so the shoulders do not stretch on a hanger.
  • Treat stains early: blot rather than rub, then follow fibre-specific care guidance.

These are general principles, not a substitute for the final garment’s instructions.
